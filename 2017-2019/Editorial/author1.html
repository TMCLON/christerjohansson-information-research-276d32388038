Instructions to Authors

Information Research: an international electronic journal attracts high usage - more than 1,700 hits a month on the top page and, probably, about 5,000 a month on the site as a whole. Some papers attract several hundred hits a month and one or two more than 1,000. Items appearing in the journal are now being cited in other, print publications and the journal is indexed in LISA and Information Science Abstracts.

Information Research is now a fully refereed journal, although we also continue to publish Working Papers, and we have four regional, international editors and an Editorial Board representative of all areas covered by the journal.

These Instructions to Authors are published so that submissions may be prepared to a standard format and style.

1. Form of submission.

Papers should be submitted electronically or on a 3" diskette in HTML format, suitable for browsing with Netscape or Internet Explorer versions 3.0 or higher (Ian S. Graham's HTML 4.0 Sourcebook, Wiley, 1998, is a useful guide for this purpose.) Any diagrams in the text should be converted to .gif or .jpg files and included in the files on the diskette or as additional attachments to the e-mail message. Word 7 for Windows converts Word files and associated diagrams or drawings very easily and effectively. The diskette should be accompanied by a paper version of the paper.

Important Warning!

Do not use Word 2000 to convert your word-processed document to HTML - Word 2000 adds a great deal of XML coding, inflating the file by 20% or more and making the page unreadable in the intended way by older browsers. I have provided a template for papers, which can be downloaded, and the easiest way to prepare your paper is to cut and paste the title, abstract, paragraphs, etc., into the template. One of our authors notes:
At this stage, I can't see how users of Word2000 can in fact avoid the addition of xml code without proofing the document code at every single stage of creating and saving a page and then MANUALLY removing the offending code.
Even Microsoft's site for Internet developers can offer no better advice:
When I have Word documents that I want to convert to minimally formatted HTML files, I save the files as text only, then I manually add just enough HTML to the output text file for the formatting I want. Sometimes this will take a little work depending on how complex the original document was, and how close to the original I want or need the resulting HTML to be.
The lesson here is to use an HTML editor, such as Homesite, not a conversion program.

2.1 In-text headings

Headings in the text should use the following HTML tags: Heading 2 for first level headings, Heading 3 for second level, and Heading 4 for third level. The heading, "References" at the end of the paper should be Heading 2.

The relevant HTML tags:

<H2>This is heading 2</H2>

<H3>This is heading 3</H3>

<H4>This is heading 4</H4>

result in the following layout:

This is heading 2

This is heading 3

This is heading 4

3. In-text citations and bibliographical references

3.1 Citations

Citations in the text should be according to the "Harvard" or "author/date" system, e.g.:

"The sample used in this study did not obey the principles of statistical sampling, but the principles of maximum variation sampling, as defined by Patton (1990) and those of theoretical sampling, as defined by Strauss & Corbin (1990), i.e., sampling on the basis of concepts that have proven theoretical relevance to the evolving theory."

3.2 Bibliographical references

The corresponding references should be set out as below:

Patton, M.Q. (1990) Qualitative Evaluation and Research Methods. 2nd. Edition. London: Sage.

Strauss, A. and Corbin, J, (1990). Basics of Qualitative Research: Grounded Theory Procedures and Techniques. London: Sage.

References to journal articles should take the form:

Robertson, A.M. and Willett, P. (1993) "A comparison of spelling-correction methods for the identification of word forms in historical text databases." Literary and Linguistic Computing, 8, 143-152.

References to parts of books, such as annual reviews, take the form:

Wilson, T.D. (1994) "Information needs and uses: fifty years of progress?", in: Fifty years of information progress: a Journal of Documentation review, edited by B.C. Vickery. London: Aslib. pp. 15-51

Unpublished papers, delivered at conferences take the form:

Dervin, B. (1983) "An overview of sense-making research: concepts, methods and results to date." Paper delivered at the International Communications Association Annual Meeting, Dallas, Texas.

Papers published in conference proceedings take the form:

Bernal, J.D. (1959) "The transmission of scientific information: a user's agenda." International Conference On Scientific Information, Washington, DC, 1958. Washington, DC: National Academy of Sciences -- National Research Council. pp. 77-95
